The Discriminant: Nature of Quadratic Solutions

The Discriminant

Understanding the Nature of Quadratic Solutions

What is the Discriminant?

For a quadratic equation in standard form:

ax² + bx + c = 0

The discriminant is the expression under the square root in the quadratic formula:

Δ = b² - 4ac
Why it matters: The discriminant tells us everything about the nature and number of solutions without actually solving the equation.

The Three Cases

Δ > 0 (Positive)

Two distinct real roots

The parabola crosses the x-axis at two different points.

Example: x² - 5x + 6 = 0

Δ = 25 - 24 = 1

Solutions: x = 2 and x = 3

Δ = 0 (Zero)

One repeated real root

The parabola touches the x-axis at exactly one point (the vertex).

Example: x² - 4x + 4 = 0

Δ = 16 - 16 = 0

Solution: x = 2 (repeated)

Δ < 0 (Negative)

No real roots

The parabola doesn't cross the x-axis. Solutions are complex numbers.

Example: x² + 2x + 5 = 0

Δ = 4 - 20 = -16

Solutions: x = -1 ± 2i

Why the Discriminant Works

The quadratic formula is:

x = (-b ± √(b² - 4ac)) / 2a

The discriminant Δ = b² - 4ac appears under the square root. This is crucial because:

  • If Δ > 0: We're taking the square root of a positive number, giving us two different values when we apply the ± sign.
  • If Δ = 0: The square root is zero, so ± 0 gives us the same answer twice.
  • If Δ < 0: We can't take the square root of a negative number in the real number system, so there are no real solutions.
Geometric interpretation: The discriminant essentially measures the "distance" between the parabola's vertex and the x-axis. A positive discriminant means the vertex is beyond the x-axis, zero means it's exactly on it, and negative means it doesn't reach.
